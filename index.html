How to Tune a Song for 46 EDO

Why do imported songs sound different?

This piano roll uses 46 equal divisions of the octave (46 EDO), where each step is ~26.09 cents. The 12 standard notes are mapped to specific positions that create an interesting temperament with slightly stretched octaves and varied interval sizes.

The current mapping:

  • C = 0, C# = 5, D = 8, D# = 13, E = 16, F = 19
  • F# = 24, G = 27, G# = 32, A = 35, A# = 40, B = 43

What this temperament offers:

  • Fifths (C-G, D-A, etc.) are ~704 cents - slightly wider than pure (702 cents)
  • Major thirds (C-E, F-A, etc.) are ~417 cents - wider than both pure (386 cents) and 12-EDO (400 cents)
  • Minor thirds (C-E♭, D-F, etc.) are ~339 cents - wider than both 12-EDO (300 cents) and pure (316 cents)
  • Semitones vary dramatically: some are ~130 cents (C-C#), others only ~78 cents (E-F, B-C)
  • This creates a unique harmonic character with its own consonances and dissonances

How to adapt imported music:

  1. The mapping preserves the relative pitch relationships, but in a different temperament
  2. Some intervals may sound more consonant, others more dissonant than expected
  3. You can adjust individual notes up or down by 1-2 steps for different harmonic colors
  4. Experiment with microtonality - try notes between the standard positions!
  5. Embrace the unique character rather than trying to exactly match 12-EDO

Tip: When creating or editing music in 46-EDO, microtonal arrow indicators help identify notes between standard pitches.

46-EDO Microtonal Sequencer

A piano roll sequencer with 46 equal divisions per octave (26.09 cents per step) for xenharmonic music.

Features

  • • 8 octaves of 46-EDO tuning
  • • Import .org and .mid files
  • • 100 wavetable instruments + 6 drums
  • • Per-note velocity and pan
  • • 256 measures with loop points
